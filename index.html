We talked about the sonority hierarchy in relation to the nonce word judgments. Sonority is an acoustic property of speech sounds, and a sonority hierarchy ranks speech sounds with respect to their sonority (see Figure 1). The sonority hierarchy plays an important role in how syllables are built in languages. The materials for the nonce word experiment were taken from Daland et al. (2011).

The sentences are island constructions, and the name comes from Ross (1967). Islands are syntactic structures that seal off the elements inside them, and thus extraction (we called this movement) out of them results in decreased, sometimes complete, acceptability. The island data is from Sprouse, Wagers & Phillips (2011).

Why did I show you these examples? I wanted to show you some instances of grammar, what we call the set of rules combining basic expressions of language, in action. The judgments come to you very easily, as they do to other speakers of English and many other languages, but it is not easy to pinpoint why you have the judgments you have. I chose fairly complex phenomena on purpose to highlight the fact that the rules are unconscious, and the generalizations we make are not straightforward.

Section notes

IPA and speech sounds

Biologically, the following are parts of our speech production mechanism, with their functions:
  • Lungs: air supply.
  • Vocal folds: located in the larynx, they set the air in motion in a way that is relevant to speech production
  • Vocal tract: consists of the pharynx, oral, and nasal cavity. Located above the larynx, they are a set of filters that modify speech sounds in various ways.
Some terminology:
  • Larynx: also called the ‘sound box,’ where the vocal folds are.
  • Pharynx: a tube between the larynx and the oral cavity.
  • Glottis: the space between the vocal folds.
  • Articulators: lips, teeth, tongue, alveolar ridge, hard palate, soft palate, etc. The coordination between articulators helps us produce a range of sounds.
  • Voicing: the vocal folds can be positioned in different ways to produce different glottal states. Voiceless and voiced states are among the most common

Speech sounds can be grouped based on which articulators are used and how they are manipulated to produce a sound. This is how consonants are grouped in the IPA. Each consonant is defined by its place of articulation and manner of articulation, along with their voicing status.

References

Dobrovolsky, M. (1997). Phonetics: The sounds of Language. In O'Grady, W., & Katamba, F. (Eds.), Contemporary Linguistics (pp. 15-33). New York: St. Martin's.

Week 4

Three major parameters for characterizing vowels:

  • Height: how high the tongue body is during the articulation of the vowel?
  • Backness: how far back the tongue body is located during the articulation of the vowel?
  • Rounding: involves the lips.

Additional parameters:

  • Oral vs. nasal vowels: if the velum is lowered when the vowel is articulated, it is a nasal vowel (same logic as nasal consonants).
  • Tense vs. lax: tense vowels are produced with a placement of the tongue that results in greater vocal tract constriction. They also tend to be longer than lax vowels.
  • In English, if words are monosyllabic and don’t have a coda, they must have a tense vowel. Think about the words do and sue.

Vowel chart of English

  • Dipthongs are vowels that change quality in a single syllable. In simple vowels, the articulation stays the same in a syllable.
  • How do we decide that they are a single vowel? Dipthongs are the nucleus of one single syllable; we don’t put the components of a dipthong into separate syllables.
